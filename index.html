Research Focus

My research has focused on several key areas within the domain of large-scale language models (LLMs), spanning the entire development lifecycle to enhance their efficiency, capability, and applicability. Specifically, my work delves into the following domains:

1. Large Model Pre-training

I investigate innovative architectures, training methodologies, and data curation strategies to build robust foundational models from scratch. This includes exploring parameter-efficient training techniques, optimizing compute and data utilization, and improving model generalization across diverse domains and languages.

2. Inference Optimization

I develop and implement advanced techniques to significantly reduce computational latency and resource consumption during model deployment. My research in this area focuses on model compression (e.g., pruning, quantization), knowledge distillation, efficient attention mechanisms, and hardware-aware acceleration—enabling faster, more scalable, and cost-effective inference solutions.

3. Post-Training Processes

I explore critical stages following pre-training to better align models with specific tasks and human preferences. This includes supervised fine-tuning (SFT), reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), safety alignment to mitigate biases and harmful outputs, and robust evaluation frameworks to assess model fairness, reliability, and performance.

4. Automated Optimization of Prompts and Skills

I pioneer methods for the autonomous discovery, refinement, and management of effective prompts and reusable “skills” for large language models. This involves research into automated prompt engineering, few-shot learning, self-debugging prompts, and systems that enable LLMs to learn how to interact and reason more effectively—solving complex, multi-step tasks with minimal human intervention.

By integrating these interconnected facets, my research aims to push the boundaries of what large language models can achieve, making them more powerful, efficient, controllable, and accessible for real-world applications.
